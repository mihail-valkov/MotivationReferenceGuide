2Lie #2: The best plan wins

Planning is useful, but in complex work it is never complete. When a team treats the plan as truth, people stop thinking, stop reporting reality, and start protecting themselves. ‚úÖ Reality check

3Lie #3: The best companies cascade goals

Cascading goals can create alignment, but it also creates theatre: busywork, gaming, and ‚ÄúOKR compliance‚Äù without commitment. Morale suffers when people cannot connect work to meaning and impact. The practical fix is not more OKRs, but better context: why we are doing this, what trade-offs we made, and how success helps customers.

‚úÖ Reality check

  • My manager is effective at setting clear expectations for my work.

4Lie #4: The best people are well-rounded

‚ÄúWell-rounded‚Äù sounds fair, but it quietly tells people they are not "perfect". High-performing teams are usually built from distinct strengths: people who are spiky in different ways, combined deliberately. Morale improves when people feel valued for what they're worth not constantly judged on their weaknesses.

‚ö†Ô∏è Morale trap

People feel constantly ‚Äúnot enough‚Äù and spend energy masking weaknesses.

‚úÖ Reality check

  • In the last month(s), I have had the opportunity to use my strengths.
  • I am challenged enough to grow in my role.

1:1 Questions

  • What work makes you feel at your best here? What do you want 20% more of?

  • Where does this person create the best value? Where do you want them used more?
5Lie #5: People need feedback

Many organisations overdose on evaluation. When feedback becomes constant judgement, people get defensive, anxious, and performative. The healthier move is ‚Äúattention‚Äù: noticing what someone does well, reinforcing it, and guiding growth with care. Recognition and coaching then become morale stabilisers, not stressors.

‚ö†Ô∏è Morale trap

Constant judgement mode leads to defensiveness, anxiety, and performative conversations.

‚úÖ Reality check

People need attention (noticed at their best, guided with care).

Pulse Example items

  • My manager cares about my growth and development.
  • My manager notices when I do something well.

1:1 Questions

  • When did you feel most seen recently? When did you feel invisible?

360 Experience-based prompt

  • What does your manager (or this person) do that makes others feel valued and noticed?
6Lie #6: People can reliably rate other people

Ratings feel objective, but they invite bias, politics, and resentment. They also push people to judge traits rather than describe impact. A stronger approach is experience-based feedback: people may be wrong about your intent, but they can reliably report how your behaviour affected their clarity, speed, motivation, or safety.

‚ÄúPotential‚Äù labels create status games and boxes around identities. Teams start investing in a select few and quietly giving up on the rest. Morale suffers because people stop seeing a path. ‚ÄúBalance‚Äù is personal and vague. In practice it often turns into guilt, PR, or generic advice while work stays painful. A more actionable lens is ‚Äúlove-in-work‚Äù: increase the parts of the job that energise and fit someone‚Äôs strengths, and reduce (or redesign) the parts they dread.

New leaders often chase a checklist and accidentally perform ‚Äúleadership theatre‚Äù. The real work is influence: creating clarity, confidence, and trust. People follow distinct strengths and leaders who reduce uncertainty by making trade-offs visible, protecting focus, and being consistent.
