How to use this guide

  • Scan the legend below so the shorthand makes sense during 1:1s and debriefs.
  • For specific behaviour, pick the most likely drive(s) and skim the relevant page for suppressors, symptoms, and nudges.
  • Use the 1:1 questions to validate your hypothesis before acting.
  • Combine team mechanics and levers with systemic supports — don't rely on perks alone.

AMP

Quick drive health check

  • D1: Is the work meaningful and connected to the real world (users)?
  • D2: Are goals clear and progress visible?
  • D3: Is there space for “flow” and experimentation with fast [system] feedback and good skill↔difficulty balance?
  • D4: Is ownership clear with real authority?
  • D5: Do people feel belonging, inclusion and recognition?
  • D6: Is urgency ethical and bounded (not constantly high)?
  • D7: Is there novelty and exploration time (with guardrails like time limits, show-and-tell)?
  • D8: Is the environment blame free, and are safety nets in place so risk is tolerable?

Drive 1: Epic Meaning & Calling ✨🌍

Definition

The deep motivation that comes from believing your work matters beyond yourself—mission, ethics, societal impact, a story worth belonging to. Clear line from your effort to real-world outcomes.

What activates it

  • A clear, authentic product mission
  • Real user stories
  • Visible impact of shipped work: usage, outcomes, testimonials, support wins.
  • Values alignment, decisions and trade-offs that match the stated mission

What suppresses it (team/culture/process)

  • Work framed only in terms of revenue or deadlines
  • Too much politics
  • Metrics-only culture
  • Cynical leadership tone
  • Mission statements that don't match decisions
  • Work that feels exploitative or trivial
  • No line-of-sight to users; backlog tasks with no context
  • Lack of connection to user outcomes or societal impact
  • Roadmaps that pivot with no “why” narration, no story, no context
  • Repetitive backlog tasks with no visible story behind them
  • Overusing hero narratives - especially to mask bad processes

Symptoms when suppressed

Detachment (“just a job”), low energy, no client touchpoints, reluctance to go the extra mile, value-based conflicts.

Activities that may suffer

  • Talking to customers, cross-team initiatives, best-practice evangelism.
  • Blog posts, user documentation, or any external docs that nobody reads (believed).
  • Doing work disconnected from real user needs.
  • Supporting legacy systems without context.
  • Product evangelism
  • Indifference to product usability and quality

Systemic supports

  • Quarterly mission & vision reviews with product team(s)
  • OKRs / Goals that include user/impact related outcomes
  • Customer Experience and Discovery rituals and practices (customer calls, usability tests) with engineers present. (Using experience/adoption management platforms like Pendo)

1:1 questions direction

  • “When did our work feel most meaningful to you in the last quarter?”
  • “Which user story sticks with you?”
  • Do you feel like your work contributes to fulfilling our mission, vision, OKRs?

Anti-patterns

Purpose disconnected from reality or real product users. Hero narratives to cover bad processes.

Signals/metrics

  • # of customer touchpoints with engineers;
  • mentions of user impact in retros.
  • Quality of customer-facing systems, going the “extra mile”

Team mechanics & levers

  • Tie each epic to a user impact narrative;
  • open sessions with a 60-sec “who benefits” story
  • Bring customers to demo & invite engineers to join client demos or user feedback sessions.
  • Share customer stories and testimonials regularly.
  • Share thank-you notes and before/after screenshots.
  • Use mission footers on tickets (why this matters).
  • Frame features in terms of impact (“we're enabling this business to thrive”).
  • Impact-based recognition (stories, before/after).

Drive 2: Development & Accomplishment 🏆🧱

Definition

Motivation that comes from achieving goals, striving for mastery, improving skills, and seeing tangible progress (like completing meaningful chunks of work).

What activates it

Clear goals, short feedback loops, visible milestones, recognition of progress, value of the achievement as defined by the social environment

What suppresses it (team/culture/process)

  • Endless projects with no clear milestones
  • Vague acceptance criteria
  • Lack of recognition - add value to achievements
  • Constant firefighting.

Symptoms when suppressed

procrastination, half-finished work, lowered learning and growth ambition

Activities that suffer

Unit/integration tests, refactoring, CI/CD improvements, dependency upgrades.

Systemic supports

DoD (Definition of Done) includes tests & docs; release notes culture; credit also invisible type of work like refactoring. Learning & development budget and practices.

1:1 questions

  • “What recent win are you proud of that maybe no one noticed?”
  • “Where is progress invisible?”
  • “What achievements may make you proud?”

Anti-patterns

Only celebrating big launches; ignoring “under-the-hood” achievements.

Signals/metrics

Cycle time trends; throughput of PRs; frequency of demos and new features bragging

Team mechanics & levers

  • Skill ladders & growth plans: clear public levelling ladders; quarterly self-assessment & manager 1:1.
  • Slice to MMFs: break initiatives into milestone-ready Minimal Marketable Features with Definition of Success - for example # of users used the feature.
  • Progress radiators like visible burn-up, ageing-WIP boards.
  • Quickly available progress charts: burn-up/burn-down, dashboards, release counters.
  • Recognition of achievement: ship reviews, “personal bests”, company-wide shoutouts, lightweight badging or leaderboards.
  • Maintain public changelogs of engineering improvements, non-engineering language

Drive 3 ‐ Empowerment of Creativity & Feedback 🎨🔁

Definition

Motivation from creative work, like coding, design, experimenting, and other flow-type of activities, and getting tight system feedback (do to confuse with social praise).

What activates it

Creative activities, fast CI, sandboxes, spikes, clear problem statements with freedom in solution.

What suppresses it (team/culture/process)

Slow feedback like Long/fragile CI, punitive response to failed experiments, heavy change control, rigid templates, bureaucracy

Symptoms when suppressed

Stagnation, lack of “inspiration”, avoidance of POCs, fear of proposing ideas, boredom, technical abilities do not correspond to the task - too-hard/too-easy

Activities that suffer

Work feels like grinding, POCs, new tooling proposals, testing culture, CI/CD work, exploratory research

Systemic supports

Distraction free environment, experimentation budget

1:1 questions

“Where do you wish you could experiment more?” “What feedback loop is too slow?”

Anti-patterns

Shaming failed spikes; conflating experimentation with recklessness.

Signals/metrics

CI duration; # of spikes/POCs; lead time for change.

Team mechanics & levers

  • Optimize daily work for Flow: calibrate challenge to skill, tasks difficulty slightly above current ability.
  • PR procedure as lightweight as possible, code reviews with fast turnaround, clear guidelines, fast feedback
  • Create POCs, prototypes/spikes for new functionality; allocate 10% time for example
  • Break work into clear milestones and short “win states” (DoD, definition of success).
  • Tight feedback loops: CI/CD
  • Quickly available build lights
  • Skill ladders & practice: pair/mob sessions, rotation for stretch tasks.
  • Anti-grind rules: cap WIP, protect focus time, avoid vanity metrics.

Drive 4: Ownership & Possession 🔑📦

Definition

Motivation from feeling that something belongs to you ‐ code, systems, ideas, decisions, outcomes ‐ and that you are responsible for it.

What activates it

Clear ownership of work domains, decisions

What suppresses it

Frequent team reshuffles, override of local decisions, communal code with no accountability.

Symptoms

“Not my problem” attitudes, slow reviews, minimal initiative, defensive behaviour.

Activities that suffer

Code reviews, maintenance, architecture docs, best-practice adoption.

Systemic supports

Ownership in on-call and backlog; platform budgets for maintenance.

1:1 questions

“What part of the system do you feel you truly own?” “Where do you feel powerless?”

Anti-patterns

Forced stewardship especially of low quality or legacy systems; Ownership without authority; authority without accountability.

Signals/metrics

Review latency by component; orphaned modules; maintenance debt.

Team mechanics & levers

  • Clear ownership maps: RACI, on-call rotations, common code ownership rules.
  • Visible asset registries: repos/services, SLAs, runbooks, dashboards.
  • Repair incentives: budget/time for tech debt & refactors in owned areas.
  • Earned privileges: decision & access rights.
  • Succession: pairing, docs, and “stewardship handover” to reduce bus factor.
  • Recognition: highlight maintainers; periodic ownership reviews.

Drive 5 ‐ Social Influence & Relatedness🤝🙌

Definition

Motivation from belonging, peer respect, collaboration, healthy competition, and social proof.

What activates it

Psychological safety, peer kudos, pairing/mentoring, inclusive rituals.

What suppresses it

Toxic (peer) competition, isolation (remote work without connection), management “favorites”, blaming/shaming culture.

Symptoms

Silence in meetings, camera-off disengagement, reluctance to ask for help, gossip.

Activities that suffer

Pairing, mentoring, team meetings, cross-team work, knowledge sharing.

Systemic supports

Onboarding buddies; communities of practice; inclusive meeting norms, establish disagree & commit; model vulnerability; protect quiet voices.

1:1 questions

“Who energises you to work with?” “When did you feel most included/excluded recently?”

Anti-patterns

“Brilliant jerk” tolerance; reward hoarding; performative “team-building”.

Signals/metrics

Participation rates; peer recognition volume; onboarding time-to-productivity.

Team mechanics & levers

  • Make excellence visible: demos, internal-media showcases, internal talks.
  • Peer recognition: kudos channels, thank-you notes, peer bonuses.
  • Mentoring structures: guides/coaches/buddies, guilds/chapters, pair/mob programming.
  • Social proof for change: champions, case studies, adoption maps.
  • Norms & rituals: ceremonies—model from leads.
  • Inclusive practices: rotate speaking, amplify quiet voices (in meetings/rituals).
  • Team-building activities.
  • Guardrails: avoid shaming or any psychological safety harming norms; measure collaboration outcomes; careful inter-team competition.

Drive 6: Scarcity & Impatience ⏳⏱

Definition

Motivation created by urgency, exclusivity, deadlines, or limited opportunities. Powerful but short-term.

What activates it

Time-boxes, limited slots, launch windows, leaderboard challenges.

What suppresses it (i.e., when it loses effect)

Perpetual crunch, fake deadlines, always-on emergencies (leading to de-sensitisation).

Symptoms of overuse

Burnouts, cynicism about deadlines, cutting corners, quality debt.

Activities affected (when misused)

Bug fixing, tech support quality, perf testing—quality collapses under chronic urgency.

Systemic supports

Capacity planning; on-call shifts; incident budgets.

1:1 questions

“Which deadline felt meaningful vs manufactured?” “Where is urgency hurting quality?”

Anti-patterns

Permanent war-room; prestige via overwork.

Signals/metrics

Change fail rate; incident recurrence; PTO usage.

Team mechanics & levers

  • Legitimate scarcity: windowed / release trains, respect capacity by meticulous prioritization.
  • Timeboxing: sprints, spikes, decision deadlines, release deadlines.
  • Access tiers: early-access groups, feature flags by cohort
  • Build legitimate sense of urgency - (business-)competition monitoring, track total cost and time-to-market, Cost of Delay (CoD)
  • Queues & reservations: ticket SLAs, signup caps.
  • Transparency: team capacity metrics, publish why limits exist; never simulate fake scarcity
  • Swap fear-based urgency with meaning + empowerment where possible
  • Guardrails: monitor stress, rotate on-call; avoid chronic urgency, avoid prolonged war-room modes

Drive 7: Unpredictability & Curiosity 🎁🎲

Definition

Motivation from novelty, surprise, exploration, and learning.

What activates it

Novelty, hackdays/atons, trying new tools, exploratory research, randomness

What suppresses it

Rigid routines, no space for play, anti-experiment culture.

Symptoms

Boredom, low initiative, resistance to process, “groundhog day” sprints.

Activities that suffer

POCs, research spikes, innovation proposals.

Systemic supports

Innovation budget; rotation program; internal conference.

1:1 questions

“What's something new you wish we tried?” “Which part of the stack are you curious about?”

Anti-patterns

Novelty for novelty's sake; starting without finishing.

Signals/metrics

# of experiments; internal talks; diversity of tools used.

Team mechanics & levers

  • Time-boxed discovery sprints/spikes; demo what you learned.
  • Randomised pairing/review partners to mix perspectives.
  • Cross-team demos/showcases - curious “how they do it”.
  • Hackathons of any sorts
  • Exploration time quota (5‐20%) - new features or tools, with show-and-tell at the end
  • Rotation of domains of work
  • Novelty in usual ceremonies - Invite demos of experiments; archive learnings
  • Guardrails: explicit objectives, limit “in-progress” state

Drive 8: Loss & Avoidance 🚧🚦

Definition

Motivation to avoid risk, blame, loss of status, or harm. Necessary for safety; harmful when dominant.

What activates it (in a healthy way)

Clear guardrails, rollback plans, incident drills, psychological safety.

When it acts like a strong suppressor of all motivation

Blame or shame culture, high stakes with no safety nets, punishments for not achieving stretch goals

Symptoms

Analysis paralysis, fear of deployment, defensive code reviews, bad vibe and silence in retros.

Activities that suffer

On-call, bug-fixing, reviews, refactors, cross-team changes.

Systemic supports

Blameless culture, Incident review rituals; error budgets; safety checklists.

1:1 questions

“What feels risky right now?” “Where would a safety net help you move faster?”

Anti-patterns

Public shaming; hero worship after outages; hiding incidents, pay tied to OKRs (stretch goals). Use PIPs (personal improvement plan) only as a pre-fire step.

Signals/metrics

Time-to-ack/resolve; postmortem quality; error rates.

Team mechanics & levers

  • Documented war-mode routine.
  • Clear escalation paths.
  • Blameless pre-mortems/post-mortems with priority actions.
  • Runbooks, backups (regularly tested), rollback drills, “fire” drills.
  • Circuit breakers & “blast-radius” limits for changes.
  • Healthy on-call: fair rotation, load metrics.
  • Guardrails: Track time/costs spent in safeguarding, avoid “feature freezes”

Drive Book / AMP short version

AMP ‐ Autonomy (A)

Definition: Desire to have agency over one's work—methods, tools, and priorities.

Activators: Choice in approach; decision rights; flexible hours; trusted async workflows.

Suppressors: Micromanagement; rigid rules; approvals for every change; top-down tasking without context.

Symptoms: Passive compliance, minimal initiative, resentment, slow problem escalation.

Activities that suffer: Design/architecture, tooling proposals, pairing, cross-team work.

Leadership nudges: Define guardrails not gates; delegate decisions explicitly; publish decision logs; ask for proposals instead of prescribing solutions.

Maps to Octalysis: D3 (ECF), D4 (OP), D7 (UC) - free to be curious.

1:1 questions: “Where do you want more “say”?”; “Which rules limit or slow you down the most?”

AMP ‐ Mastery (M)

Definition: The urge to get better at things that matter—skills, craft, problem-solving.

Activators: Stretch tasks with support; feedback loops; mentorship; time for learning.

Suppressors: Repetition, no feedback, no learning time, fear of failure.

Symptoms: Stagnation, quality drift, low pride in work, resistance to reviews.

Activities that suffer: Tests, refactoring, performance work, knowledge sharing.

Leadership nudges: Personal growth plans; pair with experts; internal tech talks; explicit time for learning.

Maps to Octalysis: D2 (DA), D3 (ECF), sometimes D5 (SIR) via mentorship.

1:1 questions: “What skill are you hungry to improve?” “Where do you get the best feedback on your craft?”

AMP ‐ Purpose (P)

Definition: Desire to be part of something larger and meaningful; to see one's work matter.

Activators: User contact; outcome stories; mission narrative; ethical alignment.

Suppressors: Feature factory mentality; revenue-only framing; opaque decision-making.

Symptoms: Cynicism, disengagement, minimal initiative, quicker turnover intent.

Activities that suffer: Customer interactions, documentation, cross-team initiatives, culture work.

Leadership nudges: Start epics with who benefits; invite customers to demos; publish “impact posts”; align work with values.

Links to Octalysis: D1 (EMC), D5 (SIR), sometimes D8 (LA) when ethics and safety are key.

1:1 questions: “When did your work last feel meaningful?” “Which users do you think about when coding?”

⚙ Mechanics of Drive Strengths (Push vs Pull)

Drives don't just “exist,” they exert force within us. Each drive creates a motivational vector that can be strong or weak depending on:

  • Number of drives engaged simultaneously - the more drives - more motivational strength
  • Depth of activation (surface vs profound) - this also depends on personal values, beliefs, personality and other factors. See Of Carrots and Sticks

Balance between White Hat (uplifting) vs Black Hat (urgent, stressful) - too much black hat activation can diminish motivation or turn to obsession

  • Friction factors - each drive can also exert friction - negative motivation for example - related to ethics, cost, risk, skill needed, access, etc.

Positive synergy

When multiple drives align, motivation skyrockets (e.g. Epic Meaning + Social Influence + Accomplishment = movements like open source).

Negative drag

When Loss/Avoidance (D8) or Scarcity (D6) outweigh, they suppress action—even if other drives are strong.

Mechanics in action

🧱 Example 1: LEGO vs Giant LEGO (for a higher cause campaign for example)

Building LEGO at home

  • D3 (Creativity), D7 (Curiosity), D4 (Ownership of “my set”).
  • Low barrier, high flow → accessible to millions.

Building a giant LEGO structure for a cause

  • Adds D1 (Epic Meaning), D2 (Achievement), D5 (Social), even D6 (Scarcity—“one-time event”).
  • BUT: D8 (Loss/Avoidance) looms large → cost, risk, time → prevents most people from acting.
  • Net result: only few people do it, but those who do are deeply motivated.

🌱 Example 2: Contributing to Open Source

Drives engaged:

  • D1 (Epic Meaning—helping the community)
  • D2 (Achievement—GitHub stars, merges)
  • D3 (Creativity—solving hard problems)
  • D5 (Social Influence—recognition by peers)

Friction / Demotivation:

  • D8 (Fear of rejection, exposure of “bad” code)
  • D6 (Time scarcity—competing with job, family)

Result: Only a fraction of developers consistently contribute, even though many would like to.

🎮 Example 3: Playing a Video Game vs Building One

Playing:

  • D7 (Unpredictability), D3 (Creativity & Feedback), D2 (Accomplishment), D5 (Social multiplayer).
  • D8 (Loss) causes friction if cost of playing the game is high
  • Easy access, instant gratification.

Making a game:

  • Adds D1 (Meaning), D4 (Ownership), D2 (Mastery).
  • BUT: D8 (High risk of failure, sunk cost) and D6 (limited time/resources) → big deterrent.

Thus, millions play games, but relatively few build them.

📝 Example 4: Writing a Blog Post vs Publishing a Book

Blog Post:

  • D3 (Creativity), D2 (Quick accomplishment), D5 (Sharing with peers).
  • Low D8 risk.

Book:

  • Adds D1 (Legacy, impact), D4 (Ownership), D2 (Major milestone).
  • But D8 (fear of failure, wasted years, criticism) + D6 (massive time need) = steep drop-off in participants.

🎤 Example 5: Giving a Short Talk vs Organising a Conference or Training

Short Talk at Meetup:

  • D2 (achievement), D5 (social recognition), D3 (creativity).
  • Manageable D8 risk.

Conference Organizer:

  • Adds D1 (meaning, impact on community), D4 (ownership), D2 (huge accomplishment).
  • But D8 (risk of failure, financial loss, stress) is massive → very few volunteer.

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • The strength of a drive = pull minus friction.
  • When multiple White Hat/Intrinsic drives align, motivation is strong—but heavy Black Hat (loss, risk, scarcity) can suppress even powerful pulls.
  • Leaders should watch where a team member is “stuck”: often it's not lack of meaning or creativity, but fear of failure, risk, or cost (D8). Removing friction can unleash suppressed drives.

Practical exercises and examples

📘 Scenario 1: “The Reluctant Code Reviewer”

What's Observed

Alex, a senior engineer, consistently delays reviewing teammates' merge requests—sometimes for days—despite being proactive on their own tickets.

Motivation Analysis:
  • Drive: Autonomy / Mastery
  • Octalysis: Loss & Avoidance (fear of conflict or being wrong), Ownership (not seeing others' code as “theirs”)
Possible Root Causes:
  • They may not feel ownership over team code quality.
  • May fear judgment or lack confidence in review authority.
  • May be avoiding interpersonal tension or unclear expectations.
Nudges:
  • Frame code review as a core area of team mastery.
  • Create a shared team norm or checklist to reduce friction.
  • Pair on reviews with juniors to foster ownership and leadership mindset.

📘 Scenario 2: “The Feature Hoarder”

Whats Observed

Dani always volunteers for high-visibility features and new technologies, but avoids writing documentation or supporting junior devs.

Motivation Analysis
  • Drive: Mastery / Purpose (selectively)
  • Octalysis: Empowerment of Creativity & Feedback, Status, Scarcity
Possible Root Causes
  • Craves growth and public accomplishment.
  • Sees mentoring and documentation as low-status or unrewarding.
  • May not understand how those activities contribute to team goals.
Nudges
  • Reframe support activities as mastery-building (e.g. “the best engineers document what they learn”).
  • Create rituals to celebrate those who support others.
  • Rotate roles with visibility to balance recognition.

📘 Scenario 3: “The Invisible New Joiner”

What's Observed

Three weeks in, Maria hasn't spoken in retros or team planning. She delivers quality code, but rarely contributes ideas or concerns.

Motivation Analysis
  • Drive: Autonomy / Relatedness
  • Octalysis: Unpredictability (new context), Avoidance, Social Influence
Possible Root Causes
  • Social safety is low, or unclear expectations.
  • Uncertain whether her input is valued.
  • May come from a culture where junior input wasn't welcome.
Nudges
  • Assign a buddy who actively invites her input.
  • Use async channels (e.g., Slack threads) to lower the pressure of a meeting
  • Ask for suggestions before meetings.

📘 Scenario 4: “The Burned-Out Hero”

What's Observed

Petar works long hours, fixes production issues alone, and rarely takes PTO. Recently, he's become irritable and withdrawn in meetings.

Motivation Analysis
  • Drive: Purpose / Mastery → drifting to Loss Avoidance
  • Octalysis: Ownership & Possession, Development & Accomplishment → Scarcity, Avoidance
Possible Root Causes
  • Feels indispensable or overly responsible.
  • May be trapped in “if I don't do it, it won't get done right” mindset.
  • Rewards (praise, status) might have reinforced unhealthy patterns.
Nudges
  • Create backup systems or rotate on-call to reduce pressure.
  • Have a direct 1:1 to validate effort and flag sustainability.
  • Share team health metrics to frame overwork as a risk.
  • Ask teammates to chat with him and possibly neutralize the “it won't get done right” thinking

📘 Scenario 5: “The Anti-Process Voice”

Observed

Mitko pushes back on every new tool or process (new sprint template, OKRs, even retros). Often says things like “this is just bureaucracy.”

Motivation Analysis
  • Drive: Autonomy
  • Octalysis: Empowerment, Scarcity (time), Unpredictability
Possible Root Causes
  • Sees top-down changes as a threat to autonomy.
  • May have prior experience with ineffective or shallow processes.
  • May feel excluded from the design of the changes.
Nudges
  • Involve them early in shaping processes—turn them into a co-owner.
  • Ask: “What's the minimal version of this that would work for us?”.
  • Show how the change increases—not reduces—team autonomy or mastery.

⚠ Pitfalls to Watch For When Using the Framework

Pitfall

Applying this guide in “one-size-fits-all” manner

Mitigation

People's motivation is not a constant and depends heavily on many factors – like stage in life, current situation and context – so leaders should approach it all individually and be adaptive

Pitfall

Over–psychologising behaviour

Mitigation

Anchor in observable facts and don't assume internal state—frame everything as hypotheses and test them

Pitfall

Binary thinking ("they're either motivated or not")

Mitigation

Note that multiple drives are always at play; also motivation ≠ happiness or comfort

Pitfall

Jumping straight to incentives

Mitigation

Nudge toward shaping context, not just adding carrots - always remember the scientifically proven 7 deadly flaws of carrots and sticks from the book Drive
