Effective Leadership: Google Project Oxygen Findings, Deconstructed for New Tech Leads

 

What it means to be an effective lead in the tech industry and how to get there?

Google's Project Oxygen identified 10 behaviours of highly effective managers. This guide translates them into practice for our Upskill Tech Leadership course: what they look like, which frameworks to use, how to learn them, and where they live in the curriculum.

1Be a good coach

One of the most critical aspects of effective leadership, particularly for new tech leads, is the ability to act as a good coach. This goes beyond simply assigning tasks or dictating solutions; it involves fostering an environment of growth, empowering team members, and guiding them towards their full potential. A good coach understands that their role is not to have all the answers, but to help their team discover their own.

💡 What it means in practice

  • Hold regular 1:1s (weekly/biweekly) focused on the person, not just tasks.
  • Ask more, better questions (curiosity first), then help them choose a path.
    • Stay away from directly solving coachee's problems or suggesting actions
    • Adapt depending on seniority and situation
  • Set clear next steps and follow up (coaching without follow-up is more like cheerleading).
  • Spot patterns: strengths, blockers, mindsets; remove obstacles or escalate

🛠️ Frameworks & tools

  • GROW (Goal → Reality → Options → Way forward) for 1:1s.
  • Active listening (reflect, label, check).
  • SBI (Situation – Behavior – Impact) for specific feedback.
  • Strengths-based coaching (use coachee strengths / Clifton strengths model).
  • AI Coaching simulator – provided in the course

📈 Level & learning sequence

Learn now (0–90 days): GROW basics, 1:1 cadence, SBI.

Build next: Patterns across people; coaching for performance & autonomy.

Deepen: Cross-team coaching; coaching other managers.

⚠️ Personality watch-outs

  • Goal-oriented: "Just tell them what to do." → Stay in questions first mode.
  • People-oriented: Comfort over candor. → Pair care and challenge (Radical Candor).
  • Tech-challenge: Turn coaching into tutorials. → Ask before showing; teach once, then observe.

2Empower the team without micromanaging

Effective tech leadership hinges on empowering teams without micromanaging. Empowerment means providing autonomy, resources, and support, fostering innovation and ownership, which leads to increased engagement, satisfaction, and better project outcomes. Conversely, micromanagement – excessive control – erodes trust, stifles creativity, and demotivates, leading to inefficiency, slow reactions, and high turnover.

💡 What it means in practice

  • Assess → Adapt → Collaborate per person/task:
    • Assess skill & motivation for this task.
    • Adapt stance (Delegate / Coach / Mentor / Teach / Direct).
    • Collaborate ‐ check-ins, DoD, follow processes, team rituals.
  • Set outcomes, not step-by-step instructions; agree checkpoints; remove blockers.

🛠️ Frameworks & tools

  • Situational/Adaptive Leadership (Hersey–Blanchard; Skill/Will matrix).
  • Delegation levels (Tell→Sell→Consult→Agree→Delegate).
  • Working agreements (how we decide/commit/escalate).

📈 Level & learning sequence

Learn now: Adaptive stance basics; two tasks delegated with guardrails.

Build next: Broaden delegation across team; make it routine.

Deepen: Delegate cross-team initiatives.

⚠️ Personality watch-outs

  • Goal-oriented: Command & control; skip coaching. → Use decision logs & trade-offs.
  • People-oriented: Vague asks. → Always end with What/Who/When.
  • Tech-challenge: Keep critical path. → Don't own hero tasks; pair-coach instead.

3Create a team environment that values everyone

Building a high-performing team requires fostering an inclusive environment where individuals feel valued and respected. This involves promoting psychological safety, enabling team members to express ideas, ask questions, and admit mistakes without fear.

Key elements for this include:

  • Active Listening & Empathy
  • Fairness & Transparency
  • Recognition & Appreciation
  • Open Communication & Feedback
  • Actively recruit diverse teams in terms of personality and ensure all voices are heard and valued.

💡 What it means in practice

  • Ensure voice equity: quieter folks get surfaced; no idea is mocked.
  • Model vulnerability (admit mistakes); respond to risks with curiosity, not punishment.
  • Inclusion in rituals: "risk round" in stand-up, "mistake of the week," decision log so commitments are transparent.
  • Build psychological safety
  • Understand and respect differences in personalities

🛠️ Frameworks & tools

  • Psychological Safety (Edmondson).
  • Five Dysfunctions (Lencioni) to diagnose trust/conflict/accountability.
  • SCARF (Status, Certainty, Autonomy, Relatedness, Fairness) to reduce threat.
  • DISC / RYGB (Surrounded By Idiots)

📈 Level & learning sequence

Learn now: Build safety in rituals; ban blamey language and behavior.

Build next: Team norms & decision hygiene. Psych safety survey/check.

Deepen: Facilitate conflict productively across teams.

⚠️ Personality watch-outs

  • Goal-oriented: Speed over voice → missed risks.
  • People-oriented: Harmony over truth → faux commitment.
  • Tech-challenge: Nitpicky reviews → silence from juniors.

4Focus on productivity and results

Shift from “busy” to valuable. Tie work to clear outcomes. Create predictable results, make throughput visible, ensure fewer nasty surprises, and less "heroic" sprints.

💡 What it means in practice

  • Move from busy to valuable: connect work to outcomes.
  • Track a small, stable set of health + delivery metrics; review weekly / bi-weekly.
  • Remove systemic friction (review cycle time, flaky tests, unclear ownership).
  • Nurture productivity-increasing learning, practices, processes and tools

🛠️ Frameworks & tools

  • Flow metrics (WIP, cycle time, throughput).
  • DORA metrics (deploy frequency, lead time, MTTR, change fail %).
  • Constraints thinking (find the bottleneck, fix the system).
  • Decision/commitment logs to avoid re-deciding.

📈 Level & learning sequence

Learn now: 1–2 delivery + 1 quality + 1 people metric; simple dashboard.

Build next: Use pre-mortems/post-mortems to learn.

Deepen: Tie metrics to business outcomes with PM.

⚠️ Personality watch-outs

  • Goal-oriented: Overfit to date; quality debt.
  • People-oriented: Avoid tough trade-offs.
  • Tech-challenge: Gold-plate internals; ignore outcomes.

5Communicate effectively

New tech leads need to learn strong communication skills to ensure their messages are clear and acted upon. This involves being fast, concise, precise, and targeted to the right audience through appropriate channels. They also need to use the right tone to build trust and adapt it for different situations, as well as motivational language to inspire their teams.

💡 What it means in practice

  • Choose async vs sync wisely; write clearly; close the loop ("what/why/next").
  • Upward: no surprises, propose options/trade-offs.
  • Sideways: avoid gossip; push issues to the right forum.
  • Downward: translate strategy into clear goals & constraints.
  • Use motivational reframing and language

🛠️ Frameworks & tools

  • Decision record format: Context → Options → Decision → Implications → Owners.
  • "What/Who/When" meeting close;
  • RACI for ownership.
  • Use SCQA structure, Pyramid Principle or similar for clear concise communication.

📈 Level & learning sequence

Learn now: Weekly written updates; decision summaries.

Build next: Rewrite status meetings as risk/decision reviews.

Deepen: Written strategy memos; cross-team briefs.

⚠️ Personality watch-outs

  • Goal-oriented: Broadcasts, not dialogues.
  • People-oriented: Over-consults; slow.
  • Tech-challenge: Jargon-dense; excludes non-engineers.

6Support career development and discuss performance

Keep growth live, not annual. Use 1:1s to set a strength-aligned growth goal, shape real practice opportunities (pairing, rotation), and give specific, timely feedback. Separate coaching conversations from assessment. Address underperformance early and humanely with clear expectations and support.

💡 What it means in practice

  • Give specific, timely feedback (SBI); recognise progress.
  • Keep growth plans alive (strengths, goals, practice opportunities).
  • Distinguish coaching vs assessment conversations; be transparent.

🛠️ Frameworks & tools

  • GROW for development goals.
  • Career ladders + skill matrices; pairing plan.
  • Radical Candor (care personally, challenge directly).
  • Start–Stop–Continue in retros/1:1s.

📈 Level & learning sequence

Learn now: Quarterly growth goal per person; monthly check-ins.

Build next: Pairing & mentorship loops.

Deepen: Promotion leveling across org, performance improvement plans (PIPs).

⚠️ Personality watch-outs

  • Goal-oriented: Only outcome feedback; little development.
  • People-oriented: Avoid tough feedback; “nice but vague.”
  • Tech-challenge: Overweight technical over cross-functional skills.

7Implement a clear vision and strategy

Translate company direction into team-level outcomes. Articulate to the team in an understandable and motivating form: the few goals we'll pursue and why, success signals, and constraints (budget, security, deadlines).

💡 What it means in practice

  • Build Shared Purpose, Vision & Goals: Clearly articulate the company and team's mission, vision, and goals.
  • Translate company direction into team-level objectives (what we will achieve and why).
  • Define constraints – budget, security, deadlines, risk tolerance, etc.
  • Align scope with PM; ruthlessly de-prioritise work that doesn't advance the vision. Priority should ensure focus.

🛠️ Frameworks & tools

  • Objectives & Key Results (OKRs)
  • Strategy reporting (Context → Problem → Options → Chosen bet → Risks).
  • Roadmap as sequence of bets, not a promised feature list.
  • Cynefin framework to sense complexity (complicated vs complex).

📈 Level & learning sequence

Learn now: One-pager: team mission, 2–3 outcomes, constraints.

Build next: Quarterly outcomes with PM.

Deepen: Portfolio view; manage trade-offs across teams.

⚠️ Personality watch-outs

  • Goal-oriented: Dates over direction; thrash.
  • People-oriented: Tries to please; loses focus.
  • Tech-challenge: Internals as strategy; outcomes fade.

8Have key technical skills

You don't need to be the top coder; you need credible judgement. Facilitate design and resource decisions, review for risk and outcomes, and stay close to operability.

💡 What it means in practice

  • Maintain credible technical judgement (architecture basics, quality, debugging, operability).
  • Do thin slices of hands-on work to stay current; don't own the critical path.
  • Understand the tech domain well enough to make quick scope vs. effort decisions
  • Read code & designs efficiently; review for risk/outcomes, not style.

🛠️ Frameworks & tools

  • Architecture decision records (ADRs) or similar.
  • Observability/SRE basics (SLOs, error budgets).
  • Design review checklist (inputs/outputs, failure modes, trade-offs).

📈 Level & learning sequence

Learn now: System map, on-call shadowing, one small recurring technical task.

Build next: Facilitate design reviews; teach fundamentals.

Deepen: Participate in building cross-team technical strategy.

⚠️ Personality watch-outs

  • Goal-oriented: Skip risk thinking; push dates.
  • People-oriented: Defer too much; low technical bar.
  • Tech-challenge: Manager on paper – becomes the super-IC again.

9Collaborate across the organisation

Your role involves facilitating collaboration and translating the meaning, vision, objectives, risks, and value into actionable tasks for all internal teams and, at times, customers involved in the production process.

💡 What it means in practice

  • Understand the business in depth.
  • Map stakeholders (PM, Design, QA, SRE/DevOps, Data, CX, Sales) and know their part in the business and processes.
  • Agree on interfaces (what/why/who info flow, SLAs, escalation paths).
  • Use resource levers, negotiate (Scope, Time, People, Budget/Tools, Quality/Risk, Sequence/Priority); document agreements.

🛠️ Frameworks & tools

  • Use elements of classic Project Management
  • Stakeholder mapping (power/interest grid).
  • RACI / DACI / RAPID for decision/role clarity.
  • Team Topologies concepts (team APIs; platform vs stream-aligned teams).
  • Principled negotiation (interests vs positions).

📈 Level & learning sequence

Learn now: Identify 3 key stakeholders; set a standing sync with agendas.

Build next: Write one cross-team working agreement.

Deepen: Lead a cross-team initiative.

⚠️ Personality watch-outs

  • Goal-oriented: Pushy; burns bridges.
  • People-oriented: Over-accommodates; team or performance suffers.
  • Tech-challenge: Ignores non-engineering side of things.

10Make strong decisions

Making decisions is not about guessing the future or being always right as you are the boss, but a repeatable process that clarifies goals, complexity, importance, capabilities of people and systems, required information, interdependencies, analyzes impact and charts a chooses a probabilistic path with contingencies.

💡 What it means in practice

  • Choose fit-for-context: reversible vs irreversible, fast vs thorough.
  • Make trade-offs explicit; write them down.
  • Involve the right people early enough to avoid late vetoes; time-box debate.
  • Decide, disagree-and-commit and execute.

🛠️ Frameworks & tools

  • Type 1/Type 2 decisions (reversible vs not).
  • OODA loop (Observe–Orient–Decide–Act).
  • Cynefin (simple/complicated/complex/chaotic).
  • RAPID/DACI (decision rights).
  • Pre-mortems; decision journals for learning.

📈 Level & learning sequence

Learn now: Decision log cadence; pre-mortem for major items.

Build next: Clarify decision rights with PMs, etc.

Deepen: Portfolio balancing; risk appetite calibration.

⚠️ Personality watch-outs

  • Goal-oriented: Decide too fast; ignore dissent.
  • People-oriented: Analysis paralysis; endless consensus.
  • Tech-challenge: Optimize the system, miss the business.

📌Bonus: Ten Common First-Time Tech Manager Mistakes

Based on experience and various sources, here is a compiled list of most common first-time tech lead mistakes.

The Top 10 Mistakes

  1. Clinging to coding / reluctance to delegate
    New managers often keep the hero IC hat on – coding nights and "saving" projects – rather than multiplying team output. It burns you out and starves the team of growth.
  2. Avoiding (or mishandling) tough feedback
    Skipping direct, timely feedback – or routing it through leadership before speaking to the person – erodes trust and performance. New managers frequently lack training here and default to silence or vague hints.
  3. Skipping 1:1s or turning them into status updates
    1:1s are for coaching, context, and early issue detection – not checkbox status. Treating them as optional (or as JIRA recaps) is a classic early mistake.
  4. Micromanaging – or the flip side, "trusting without verifying"
    First-time managers often swing between over-control (rewriting code, prescribing every step) and abdication (no guardrails, no follow-up). You need calibrated autonomy plus clear standards.
  5. Not setting (or communicating) a simple vision and near-term priorities
    Teams stall when they can't see how work contributes. "We're busy" ≠ direction. New managers commonly do not articulate their guiding principles well enough.
  6. Withholding context from the team
    People can't make good decisions without product, customer, and business constraints. Managers who keep context up-top unknowingly throttle velocity and morale.
  7. Trying to change everything at once
    Big-bang fixes (process, tools, org) create thrash. The better path is a few visible, compounding improvements.
  8. Letting underperformance linger
    Conflict-avoidance leads to months of ambiguity that hurt the whole team. Address it early, directly, and humanely.
  9. Hiring and onboarding missteps
    Rushing hires, overweighting "mirror" candidates, and weak onboarding are expensive early errors for new managers. They ripple for quarters.
  10. Try to do it alone (no help from peers, mentors)
    First-time EMs who go it alone struggle more and longer. Deliberately assemble mentors/peers and ask for help.

How these mistakes might derail your path to being a highly performing manager

  • Mistake #1 (cling to coding) ▶️ might affect Oxygen #2 (empower, don't micromanage) and #8 (technical skills to advise). Possibly affect #3 (environment) as doing the work by themselves may signal distrust in other members.
  • Mistake #2 (avoid feedback) + #3 (skip 1:1s) ▶️ Oxygen #1 (coach) and #6 (career & performance).
  • Mistake #4 (over/under-control) ▶️ Oxygen #2 (empower with standards) and #10 (strong decisions).
  • Mistake #5 (no vision/priorities) ▶️ Oxygen #7 (vision/strategy).
  • Mistake #6 (withhold context/poor communication) ▶️ Oxygen #5 (communicate) and #3 (inclusive, safe env.).
  • Mistake #7 (change everything at once) ▶️ Oxygen #4 (results-oriented) – focus on few compounding improvements.
  • Mistake #8 (let underperformance linger) ▶️ Oxygen #6 (discuss performance) + #10 (make decisions).
  • Mistake #9 (hiring/onboarding missteps) ▶️ Oxygen #4 (results) and #6 (growth) – high-leverage manager work.
  • Mistake #10 (do it alone) ▶️ Oxygen #9 (collaborate across org) – get peers/mentors.
